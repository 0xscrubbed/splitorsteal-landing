Bill Splitting FAQ

Everything you need to know about splitting bills fairly, dealing with freeloaders, and how game theory solves the problem.

Bill Splitting Basics

What is the fairest way to split a bill?

The fairest way to split a bill is to use a system where everyone has equal say and no one can game the outcome. Split or Steal solves this with secret voting — each person privately chooses to Split (pay fairly) or Steal (try to underpay). If everyone cooperates, the bill divides evenly. If someone tries to cheat, they face an automatic Steal Tax penalty that increases their share. This removes the two biggest problems with bill splitting: the person who always underpays, and the awkwardness of calling them out.

How do you split a dinner bill without it being awkward?

The awkwardness comes from having to confront someone face-to-face about money. Split or Steal removes this entirely — everyone votes on their phone secretly, and the app reveals all votes at once. There's no pointing fingers, no "you only had a salad" debates, and no passive-aggressive Venmo requests. The game theory engine handles fairness automatically. Turn it into a fun game instead of an uncomfortable conversation.

Is splitting bills evenly actually fair?

Splitting evenly is only fair when everyone ordered roughly the same thing. In reality, someone always orders the steak while someone else had a salad. That's why Split or Steal doesn't just divide by headcount — it uses secret voting to reveal who's willing to pay their fair share and who's trying to freeload. The Steal Tax adjusts the split based on behaviour, not just arithmetic. It's fairness based on intent, not just math.

What expenses can you split with Split or Steal?

Split or Steal works for any shared expense: restaurant bills, rent and utilities, road trip costs (fuel, tolls, accommodation), concert and event tickets, group gifts, grocery runs, Uber and taxi rides, vacation expenses, streaming service subscriptions, and even bar tabs. If money is being shared between two or more people, Split or Steal makes it fair.

The Freeloader Problem

What do you do when one person always underpays at dinner?

Every friend group has one person who conveniently "forgets" their card, orders the most expensive thing, or throws in $20 on a $45 meal. Instead of confronting them (which damages friendships), use Split or Steal. The secret vote means their behaviour is exposed by the game, not by you. If they choose Steal, the Steal Tax automatically adjusts their share upward. The social pressure of being "revealed" as the Stealer usually corrects the behaviour permanently.

How is Split or Steal different from Splitwise or Venmo?

Splitwise and Venmo are calculators — they split a number and send payment requests. They don't solve the actual problem: the person who always underpays. Split or Steal is a game, not a calculator. It uses secret voting and game theory to create social accountability. You can't just throw in less money and hope nobody notices. The vote reveals everything. Think of it as the difference between a calculator and a lie detector for your dinner table.

Game Theory & How It Works

How does Split or Steal use game theory?

Split or Steal is built on the Prisoner's Dilemma — a foundational concept in game theory. Each player independently decides to cooperate (Split) or defect (Steal) without knowing what others chose. The payoff matrix is designed so that mutual cooperation (everyone Splits) produces the best collective outcome. Defecting (Stealing) while others cooperate gives a short-term advantage, but the Steal Tax penalty makes it a losing strategy over time. This creates a Nash equilibrium where splitting fairly is the dominant strategy.

What is the Steal Tax?

The Steal Tax is the penalty applied to anyone who votes Steal. When you choose Steal while others choose Split, the app increases your share of the bill. The exact amount depends on the mode — Standard mode has a moderate penalty, while Chaos Mode has a much higher Steal Tax for bigger stakes. It's what makes the game theory work: trying to underpay always costs more than just splitting fairly.

What is Chaos Mode?

Chaos Mode is the high-stakes version of Split or Steal. The Steal Tax penalty is significantly higher, making the consequences of Stealing much more dramatic. It's designed for groups who want more tension and bigger reveals — bachelor parties, end-of-year dinners, or any time the group wants to raise the stakes. The game theory still applies, but the incentive to cooperate is even stronger.
