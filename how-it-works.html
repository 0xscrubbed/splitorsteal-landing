How to Split a Bill Fairly Using Game Theory

Secret voting + the Prisoner's Dilemma = no more freeloaders.

The Problem with Splitting Bills

Every friend group has the same problem: one person always underpays. They order the most expensive thing, then suggest splitting evenly. Or they throw in $20 on a $45 meal and hope nobody counts. You know who they are. Everyone does. But nobody says anything because money conversations are awkward.

Traditional bill splitting apps just divide a number. They don't address the real issue — the freeloader. Splitwise doesn't know someone ordered the lobster. Venmo can't tell you who's been short-changing the group for years. These tools are calculators, not solutions.

Split or Steal is different. It uses game theory to make fairness automatic.

How Split or Steal Works

1

Create a game

Enter the total bill — dinner, rent, road trip, bar tab, whatever. Add everyone who's splitting. Choose Standard mode for normal splits, or Chaos Mode for high-stakes games with bigger penalties. Share the game link with your group.

2

Everyone votes secretly

Each person privately chooses: Split (pay your fair share) or Steal (try to pay less). Nobody sees what anyone else chose. A countdown timer adds pressure. This is the Prisoner's Dilemma in real life — your decision depends on what you think everyone else will do.

3

The big reveal

All votes are revealed at once. If everyone chose Split, the bill divides evenly — best outcome. If someone chose Steal, the Steal Tax kicks in and increases their share automatically. No confrontation needed. The game exposes the freeloader. The math handles the rest.

The Game Theory Behind It

Split or Steal is built on the Prisoner's Dilemma — one of the most studied problems in game theory. Two (or more) players independently choose to cooperate or defect without knowing what others chose. The tension: defecting gives you an advantage if others cooperate, but if everyone defects, everyone loses.

The payoff matrix

Others Split Others Steal
You Split Fair share — best group outcome You pay fair, they pay penalty
You Steal Steal Tax — you pay MORE Everyone pays penalty — worst outcome

Nash equilibrium

The Steal Tax is what makes this work. In a standard Prisoner's Dilemma, defection is tempting because there's no penalty. Split or Steal adds one: if you Steal while others Split, you pay more than your fair share, not less. This shifts the Nash equilibrium — the strategy where no player benefits from changing — to mutual cooperation. Splitting becomes the dominant strategy because Stealing always costs more.

Why it works in real life

Game theory works because it aligns incentives with behaviour. The freeloader in your group isn't evil — they're just responding to incentives. When there's no penalty for underpaying, the rational move is to underpay. Split or Steal changes the incentive structure. Now the rational move is to pay fairly. The game does the social enforcement that nobody wants to do in person.
